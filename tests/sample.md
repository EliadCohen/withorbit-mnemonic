# The Photoelectric Effect

The photoelectric effect is the emission of electrons from a material when light shines on it. This phenomenon was first observed by Heinrich Hertz in 1887 and later explained by Albert Einstein in 1905.

When light of sufficient frequency hits a metal surface, electrons are ejected. The key insight is that the energy of the ejected electrons depends on the *frequency* of the light, not its intensity. Higher intensity light ejects more electrons, but does not increase their individual energy.

## Einstein's Explanation

Einstein proposed that light consists of discrete packets of energy called **photons**. Each photon carries energy $E = h\nu$, where $h$ is Planck's constant and $\nu$ is the frequency of light.

For an electron to be ejected, a single photon must provide enough energy to overcome the **work function** $\phi$ of the metal — the minimum energy needed to free an electron from the surface. The kinetic energy of the ejected electron is:

$$KE = h\nu - \phi$$

This equation shows that below a certain threshold frequency, no electrons are emitted regardless of light intensity.

## Implications for Quantum Mechanics

The photoelectric effect provided crucial evidence that light has particle-like properties, challenging the classical wave theory of light. This wave-particle duality became a cornerstone of quantum mechanics.

Einstein received the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1921 specifically for his explanation of the photoelectric effect, not for his theory of relativity.
